Motivation¶

In tennis tall players are sterotypically considered big servers and bad returners. When I hear it mentioned though by commentators I rarely hear any statistics or data but just their observations. I want to find out if there really is a relation between height with both serving and returning statistics. When you think of the great servers the names that come to mind are John Isner, Karlovic, who are considered tall for tennis players, but you also think of players like Pete Sampras and Rodger Federer who are not especially tall for professional tennis players. This leads me to question whether there is a real relation between height and serving in professioal tennis or whether it is an old sterotype of tennis that is still around, based on observation and not data. I don't only want to answer whether they are bigger serves, as in they can serve it faster, but if they are more effective servers, do they win more service points then other player. There is also the idea in tennis that tall players are not as good at returning. There are examples on both sides of this that I can thinks of as well. Like John Isner is tall and not a very good returner, but on the other hand Del Potro was a tall player and a good returner. Once again I want to see if there is any statistical evidence for these claims. These observations in themselves are not evidence for or against the claim, but they did spark my interest to look deeper into this idea. Then if there is a relation between both serving and returning with height, I want to see whether it is a good trade off or a bad tradeoff by seeing what the relation with height and win rate is. This would be good to know when deciding who to look at for tennis prospects. It would be important to know for tennis coaches to know the effect height has on a players ability to both serve and return as well as win. It is also important to look at the data in sports and not get distracted by the flare of a player. Some one may have a big serve, and watching him would be impressive, but that doesn't mean they win service point more often. I feel that it is very common to make judgements on sport based on appearance and not on data, and that is why I want to take a data driven players serving abilities versus their height.

Background¶

I want to go over some of the basics of tennis first so you will be able to better understand what I am going to be talking about. If you want a more in depth look at the rules of tennis you can look here http://protennistips.net/tennis-rules/. If you want to learn more about tennis in general and playing tennis you can look at the USTA site here https://www.usta.com/. Now I will go over a brief summary of things I think you should know to understancd what I am talking about. First if you could look at this diagram of a court so you will know the terminology https://tenniscompanion.org/a-diagram-of-tennis-court-dimensions-layout/. A tennis match consists either of a best of 3 or 5 sets. A set consists of best of 6 games win by 2, but if you get to 6 all you play a tie break. For each game you have one player serving and one returning. A game is first to four points win by 2. Players alternate serve each game. A serve is the shot that starts a point where one player standing behing the baseline hits it into diagonal service box of the opponent. You get two serves in tennis, so if the 1st serve goes out you have one more chance before losing a point. If they miss the second serve then they lose a point. After each serve players switch which service box is being served to.

Terminology:
Double Fault: If you hit 1st and 2nd serve out that is called a double fault
Ace: If the server hit his serve in and it goes untouched by the returner
Break Point: If the returner is one point from winning the game
Service Box: Section of the court the serve must land in
Baseline: Is the boundary line that is parallel to the net. Hold: When server wins the game Break: When returner win the game

Data Imputation For Remaining Missing Data¶
Now I am going to fill in the missing data for the rest of the columns. These columns consist of 1st serve in for both winners and losers,1st serve won for both, aces, 2nd serves won, number of service points, break points faced and saved. The way I am going to do this is, for each player that is missing a value in one of these categories, I will calculate their average value of the statistic for when they lose and win respectively and then fill the missing value in as the average for that player. I choose to round the average since a decimal doesnt make sense as an entry for these statistics only a whole number would make sense.

# use column names to iterate through and not need to repeat code
columnNames = ['1stIn','1stWon','ace','svpt','bpFaced','bpSaved','2ndWon']
for col in columnNames:
    #fills in missing winner data
    for name in np.unique(data[data['w_'+col].isna() == True]['winner_name']):
        #get data of player thats not missing
        playerdata = data[data['winner_name'] == name]
        playerdata = playerdata[playerdata['w_'+col].isna() == False]
        #caculate average for that player
        avg = np.average(playerdata['w_'+col])
        #fill in missing data for each player with their average
        data.loc[(data['winner_name'] == name) & (data['w_'+col].isna()==True),'w_'+col] = np.round(avg)

    #fills in missing loser data
    for name in np.unique(data[data['l_'+col].isna() == True]['loser_name']):
        #get data for player that is not empty
        playerdata = data[data['loser_name'] == name]
        playerdata = playerdata[playerdata['l_'+col].isna() == False]
        #caculate the avg
        avg = np.average(playerdata['l_'+col])
        #fill in missing data for player with his average
        data.loc[(data['loser_name'] == name) & (data['l_'+col].isna()==True),'l_'+col] = np.round(avg)

Calculate Statistics/Data Analysis¶
Now that I have filled in all the missing data the table is ready for analysis and make calculations. I will use the data in the table to calculate 7 different statistics for all the players. Four will be service statistics and the other three will be return statistics. I will calculate the first serve percent, which is the percent of first serves that are in. Then I will calculate both the first serve and second serve win percent, which is how many points they are winning of there serves respectively. A double fault,when second serve goes out, will count to losing a second serve point. Finally for serve I will calculate the pertenage of serves that are aces. Now for the return statistics I will calculate the average number of break chances they had per match, when they were one point away from winning a game on return. The break points won percent, of the chances they had how many did they win. For this statistic I decided if they had 0 break point chances then the percent would also be zero, since if they never get a break chance they aren't being very successful at returning so the percent should reflect that. Then just the total percent of return points won. After we look at the individual statistics I will calculate their win percent to look at later. This makes it a total of 8 statistics.

#only want each players name once
players = np.unique(np.append(data['winner_name'],data['loser_name']))
#list for each desired statistic
winrates = []
firstPercents = []
heights = []
firstWRates = []
secWinRate = []
aceRate = []
bpChances = []
bpWinPer = []
winReturnPercents = []

#go through each player and calculate the statistics and append to the 
#corresponding list above
for player in players:
    #this adds the heights of all the player to height
    heights.append(int(np.unique(np.append(
        data[data['winner_name']==player]['winner_ht'],data[data['loser_name']==player]['loser_ht']))))
    #this next section calculate the 1st serve percent for each player
    #first seperate the data of when the player wins vs loses
    windata = data[data['winner_name']==player]
    losedata = data[data['loser_name']==player]
    #calculate total matches first this will be used in several calculations
    totalMatches = (len(windata)+len(losedata))
    #find first serves in and then total serve when won
    winServesIn = np.sum(windata['w_1stIn'])
    winServes = np.sum(windata['w_svpt'])
    #if they never won a game need to check if nan
    if winServesIn == np.nan:
        winServesIn = 0
    if winServes == np.nan:
        winServes = 0
    #find first serves in and then total serve when they loss
    loseServesIn = np.sum(losedata['l_1stIn'])
    loseServes = np.sum(losedata['l_svpt'])
    #must check if nan incase player didn't lose a game
    if loseServesIn == np.nan:
        loseServesIn = 0
    if loseServes == np.nan:
        loseServes = 0
    firstPercent = (winServesIn +loseServesIn)/(winServes+loseServes)
    firstPercents.append(firstPercent)
    
    #Now I am going to calculate the first and second serve win rate.
    firstWon = (np.sum(windata['w_1stWon']) + np.sum(losedata['l_1stWon']))
    firstWRate = firstWon/(winServesIn + loseServesIn)
    firstWRates.append(firstWRate)
    #second serves is total serves - 1st in
    secServes = (winServes - winServesIn) + (loseServes - loseServesIn)
    secWon = (np.sum(windata['w_2ndWon'])+np.sum(losedata['l_2ndWon']))
    secWinPer = secWon/secServes
    secWinRate.append(secWinPer)
    
    #Now I will calculate the ace rate
    totalAce = (np.sum(windata['w_ace']) + np.sum(losedata['l_ace']))
    totalServes = (winServes + loseServes)
    acePer = totalAce/totalServes
    aceRate.append(acePer)
    
    #Now will calculate the return stats for players
    #starting with break point chances.
    #break chances would be same as oppents break points faced 
    #so I sum that value.
    breakpoints = np.sum(windata['l_bpFaced']) + np.sum(losedata['w_bpFaced'])
    avgbp = breakpoints/totalMatches
    bpChances.append(avgbp)
    
    #There were some matches with no break points so can't divide by 0
    #Choose to append 0 as percent since if you got no break point chances
    #you weren't returning well so should have a percent to reflect that.
    if breakpoints == 0:
        bpWinPer.append(0)
    else:
        # Break points the player won calculated by the break points not saved by opponent
        bpWon = (breakpoints - (np.sum(windata['l_bpSaved']) + np.sum(losedata['w_bpSaved'])))
        bpPercent = bpWon/breakpoints
        bpWinPer.append(bpPercent)
    
    #points won on return is calculated by opponents points not won on serve.
    returnPointWon = (np.sum(windata['l_svpt'])-(np.sum(windata['l_1stWon']) + np.sum(windata['l_2ndWon'])))
    returnPointsLoss = (np.sum(losedata['w_svpt'])-(np.sum(losedata['w_1stWon']) + np.sum(losedata['w_2ndWon'])))
    totalReturnPoints = returnPointWon + returnPointsLoss
    totalOpponentServes = (np.sum(windata['l_svpt']) + np.sum(losedata['w_svpt']))
    returnWinPer = totalReturnPoints/totalOpponentServes
    
    winReturnPercents.append(returnWinPer)
    
    #Now calculate the win rates
    winrate = len(windata)/totalMatches
    winrates.append(winrate)

Hypothesis Testing¶
Now I am going to use the statistical model library to make linear regressions for the data and run a hypothesis test. First I am assuming the relation between these statistics and heights are linear. I think this is true in the scope of professional players, who have a higher cut off for height then the human population, if you extended this to a wider range of heights it might not be linear. Nothing in the plot above suggested it should be a different order polynomial to me. I think a linear relationship works.















Reminder of the assumptions of a linear regression 

1.That a linear relationship exists 

2.Independence of residuals 

3.Constant Variance of residuals (this is known as 'Homoscedasticity') 

4.That the residuals are normally distributed (this is known as 'Normality')















To do this I will being using the statisticsal model library, since they have many more built in features for hypothesis testing then other machine learning algoritms. I will be using the OLS linear regression. OLS means ordinary least squares which refers to the loss function the regression is using. I think least squares is on of the better loss functions so that is why I chose to use this version. If you want to learn more about the library or this specific regression you can use the links below.



Statistical Model: https://www.statsmodels.org/dev/user-guide.html

More on the specific regression: https://www.statsmodels.org/dev/generated/statsmodels.regression.linear_model.OLS.html

Conclusion¶
It appears that the stereotype that tall players are good servers are and bad returns have a basis in the data on the ATP tour over the last couple of years. While not all the hypothesis tests showed that height was significant, there was at least one for both serving and returning that did. It appears that height has a positive relation with first server percent in, first serves percent won, and ace rate, while having a negative relation with total return points percent won. Based on the hypothesis tests I am unable to make a conclusion for any relation between height and break point chances and break point win percentage. It would be interesting to look deeper into why that is. If you are winning less return points you would think you would average less break point chances. Maybe taller people are having a lot more return games where they only win 1 or even no points, while other players more consistantly get 2 points but not to a break point. Then they both get a similar number of games where they make it to break point and actually win. This could be one explanation for this apparent inconsistency. It also appears that height has a positive relationship with winning. Taller players tend to have a higher win percent on the ATP tour. This means the the tradeoffs in of being tall in the game of tennis are worth it overall. This is good to know especially when it comes to looking for rising talent, you might what to focus on taller players even if at the moment they are slightly worse because they have a untrainable feature that helps increase winning. Therefore if you can catch them up skill wise they could possibly be a better player. This of course is not a certain thing as we have addressed certaion outliers like Federer, Djokovic, and Nadal. Clearly height should not be the only factor taken into account but it should be thought about be the tennis community when looking for young up and comers.
